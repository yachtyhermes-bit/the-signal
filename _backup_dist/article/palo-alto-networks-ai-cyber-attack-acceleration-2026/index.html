AI Is Now the Attacker: Palo Alto Networks Just Got 5 Analyst Upgrades in 10 Days

Jefferies, Oppenheimer, and Truist all raised targets as $PANW rides a 7-day winning streak — but the real thesis is about AI compressing cyber attack timelines from months to minutes.

May 25, 2026 4 minutes

$PANW just did something that gets attention even in this market: five analyst upgrades in ten trading days. Jefferies raised its target to $265 with a thesis that AI is "compressing attack timelines." Oppenheimer went to a street-high $275. Truist maintained its Buy at $275. BTIG initiated bullish coverage. And the stock responded — a 32% surge in seven days, taking Palo Alto Networks to $260.58 and within touching distance of all-time highs.

The obvious narrative is momentum. Cyber stocks are hot — CrowdStrike, Cisco, and Palo Alto all hit records this month as "cyber stocks outrun chips" became a market meme. But the analysts pushing these upgrades aren't talking about momentum. They're talking about something more structural: AI is changing the offense-defence balance in cybersecurity for the first time in a decade.

AI isn't just defending networks anymore. It's attacking them. And the speed of AI-powered attacks is compressing response timelines from months to minutes. That's a demand catalyst that doesn't care about interest rates.

Jefferies analyst Joseph Gallo put it bluntly: AI is "compressing attack timelines," meaning the window between a vulnerability being discovered and an AI-powered exploit being weaponized is shrinking fast. Legacy point solutions can't keep up. Palo Alto's platform strategy — integrating network security, cloud security, and AI-driven threat detection into a single stack — becomes the default answer for enterprises that can't afford to be reactive.

Oppenheimer's Ittai Kidron took it a step further, tying Palo Alto's CyberArk acquisition (now rebranded as Idira) to an identity security thesis that positions $PANW as the dominant platform for "agentic identity" — securing not just human users but AI agents operating autonomously inside enterprise networks. That's a TAM expansion that didn't exist two years ago.

Here's the contrarian question the market isn't asking: at 65x forward earnings, how much of this AI attack acceleration is already priced in? The stock has doubled from its 2025 lows. Revenue is growing at 14.9%, not 50%. Even the most bullish analyst targets imply only 5-6% upside from here. The mean analyst target of $225 actually sits below the current price — meaning most analysts still see the stock as overvalued at $260.

What makes this time potentially different: the upgrade cycle is accelerating, not peaking. Oppenheimer's $275 was the street high for exactly one week before others started raising targets toward it. Morgan Stanley just lifted its target to $253. If the AI attack compression thesis is right, this is a revenue acceleration story, not just a multiple expansion story. Palo Alto's platform strategy wins when CIOs consolidate vendors — and the AI threat landscape is forcing exactly that consolidation at exactly the right time.

The Memorial Day setup is simple: $PANW reports fiscal Q3 earnings on June 2. The options market is pricing a 7-8% move. If the company delivers the kind of AI-driven demand surge the upgrades are teeing up, $275 won't be the ceiling — it'll be the floor.
