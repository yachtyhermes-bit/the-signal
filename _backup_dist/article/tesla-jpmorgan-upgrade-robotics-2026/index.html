JPMorgan Just Killed Its 3-Year Tesla Sell Call — And the New Price Target Is a Stunner

Wall Street's most persistent Tesla bear has capitulated. JPMorgan removed its multi-year sell rating on TSLA and hiked its price target 228% to $475, pointing to robotics and Optimus as the reason. The stock still dropped 10% on the day.

Jun 6, 2026 4 minutes

JPMorgan Chase just did something it hasn't done in nearly three years: it stopped telling investors to sell Tesla. On Friday, the bank removed its sell rating on TSLA — a bearish call dating back to 2023 — and upgraded the stock to Neutral with a staggering 228% price target increase to $475 per share.

The upgrade marks the end of one of Wall Street's most persistent bearish stances on Tesla. JPMorgan's sell rating had survived every rally, every product launch, and every competitor upgrade over three years. Until now.

The timing was almost cruel. On the same day the upgrade hit, Tesla shares closed at $391.00, down roughly 10.3% from the prior session's $435.79 — not because anyone doubted the call, but because the entire market was in freefall. The May jobs report showed 172,000 jobs added, stoking fears the Federal Reserve would keep rates higher for longer. The Nasdaq and S&P 500 had their worst day of the year, and chip stocks alone shed an estimated $1.3 trillion in market value.

In other words, Tesla landed one of the most bullish analyst reversals in recent memory — and the stock got crushed anyway.

The Robotics Thesis: Why JPMorgan Finally Blinked

What makes this upgrade notable isn't just the price target jump — though going from roughly $145 to $475 is eye-popping. It's the rationale. JPMorgan explicitly cited Tesla's robotics division, including the Optimus humanoid robot program, as a long-term growth driver the market is underappreciating.

For years, the bear case was simple: Tesla is a car company trading at a tech multiple, and automotive competition would eventually catch up. JPMorgan was the standard-bearer for that view. The new thesis acknowledges what bulls have argued for years — that Tesla's value increasingly lies in AI, automation, and the physical embodiment of artificial intelligence through robotics.

"TSLA is at the forefront of physical AI," JPMorgan analysts wrote, signaling a fundamental shift in how one of Wall Street's most conservative voices now values the company. The Optimus program, manufacturing automation, and the potential for robotics to become a meaningful revenue stream over the next decade were all cited as factors the old valuation framework missed.

A Bear Capitulation Three Years in the Making

The upgrade's significance goes beyond one note. JPMorgan was the last major holdout among big banks. Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, and Bank of America had all either maintained buy ratings or upgraded Tesla at various points. JPMorgan alone held the line — a stance that looked prescient during drawdowns and stubborn during rallies.

Now that line is gone. The sell rating that once predicted a 65% downside has been retired. The 228% price target increase isn't just a number — it's an acknowledgment that the framework for valuing Tesla has permanently changed.

The new $475 target implies roughly 21% upside from Friday's close, though that gap looks more theoretical than actionable given macro headwinds. Tesla's 52-week range spans from $273.21 to $498.83; the stock is down 9.16% year-to-date, up 26.84% over the trailing twelve months, and up 46.21% over three years.

The Macro Irony: Upgrade Day Meets Market Meltdown

There's a cruel irony in Tesla receiving its most significant analyst validation in years on the exact day markets suffered their worst session of 2026. The May jobs report's strong print was interpreted not as good economic news but as bad news for rate cuts. If the labor market runs too hot, the Fed has less reason to ease.

That rate sensitivity hit the entire AI and tech complex. Tesla, despite the upgrade, was swept up in indiscriminate selling. The chip sector's $1.3 trillion wipeout underscored how little an individual analyst call can do against a macro tidal wave.

For Tesla investors, the day was a frustrating mix of validation and pain. The last major bear waved the white flag, and the stock still dropped double digits. But beneath the surface, the upgrade represents something more durable than a single session: a Wall Street consensus finally, belatedly, pricing in Tesla's ambitions beyond automobiles.

What Comes Next

The upgrade to Neutral — not Buy — matters. JPMorgan isn't pounding the table. It's simply acknowledging the sell thesis no longer holds. The robotics opportunity is real but far from proven at scale, and Tesla's core automotive business still faces margin pressure, competition, and the macro headwinds that dragged shares lower Friday.

Still, when the Street's most stubborn bear capitulates, it's worth paying attention. The question isn't whether Tesla is overvalued as a car company — it's whether the market is properly valuing Tesla as a robotics and AI company that also makes cars. JPMorgan just answered with a 228% price target hike. The rest of Wall Street is watching.
