### 7. THE AFTERMARKET SERVICES MOAT — CORE THESIS

**The Razor-and-Blades Model, Industrial Scale**

GE Aerospace doesn't just sell jet engines — it sells **power-by-the-hour** service contracts that produce guaranteed recurring revenue for 20-30 years per engine.

**Deferred Revenue (Service Contracts):**
- Current Deferred Revenue (Jun 2026): **$18.1B**
- Non-Current Deferred Revenue: **$1.07B**
- **Total Deferred Revenue: ~$19.2B** — prepaid maintenance and service contracts
- This is essentially unearned revenue: customers pay upfront for TrueChoice, Time-on-Wing, and other aftermarket service agreements

**Installed Base:**
- **44,000+ engines in service** (18K commercial + 26K military per prior article)
- Each engine generates service revenue for 20-30 years
- LEAP engine deliveries are accelerating — every new LEAP delivered adds ~$20M+ in lifetime service revenue

**CFM Partnership:**
- CFM International is a 50/50 joint venture between GE Aerospace and Safran Aircraft Engines
- Produces the **LEAP engine family** — exclusive power for Boeing 737 MAX, one of two options for Airbus A320neo
- Together they hold ~60% of the narrowbody engine market
- LEAP backlogs extend through the 2030s

**Competitive Advantage vs Pratt & Whitney:**
- Pratt & Whitney's GTF (geared turbofan) has suffered major durability issues — hundreds of A320neo aircraft grounded for premature gearbox inspections
- Airlines are increasingly favoring LEAP-powered A320neos over GTF-powered ones
- This is driving incremental market share gains for CFM/LEAP

### 10. KEY RISKS
1. **Valuation risk** — 40x forward earnings leaves little room for error; PEG of 8.4x suggests the market already prices in rapid growth
2. **Vernova spin-off residual** — GE still carries insurance liabilities from the old GE Capital days ($4.3B in insurance/claims liabilities)
3. **Boeing exposure** — Boeing 737 MAX production woes directly impact LEAP deliveries
4. **Supply chain** — titanium and specialty alloy shortages could constrain engine production
5. **Air traffic normalization** — a recession would reduce flight hours, which directly impacts aftermarket service revenue (the lifeblood of the model)
6. **LEAP transition to RISE** — CFM's next-gen RISE open-fan architecture is due by 2035; there's technology risk in the transition

### 11. THE VERNOVA SPIN-OFF RESIDUAL
- GE Vernova (energy) was spun off in April 2024
- Remaining GE Aerospace is **pure-play aerospace** — no more conglomerate discount
- However, ~$4.3B in insurance liabilities remain on the balance sheet from old GE Capital
- These are run-off liabilities and declining over time

**Core Thesis:** 
GE Aerospace is often framed as an engine manufacturer, but its real business is aftermarket services. With $19.2B in deferred service revenue, a 44,000+ engine installed base that grows every quarter, and the LEAP engine franchise gaining share from Pratt & Whitney's GTF troubles, GE is executing a textbook razor-and-blades model at industrial scale. The services business drives 20.6% operating margins, 48.2% ROE, and a FCF machine that generated $8.4B in the last twelve months despite CapEx of barely 2.5% of revenue. The bull case isn't about engine deliveries — it's about the compounding service revenue from every LEAP engine that's already flying.

**Key Competitive Insight:**
Pratt & Whitney's GTF durability crisis is a gift to GE/CFM. Every A320neo order that shifts from GTF to LEAP is a 30-year service revenue stream. Meanwhile, the installed base widens the moat every quarter — there is no second-place winner in aftermarket services for an engine you already own. Once a GE engine is on the wing, the service contract belongs to GE for the life of that aircraft.
